1. Understanding the Business

The foundation starts with deeply understanding the business—its goals, challenges, and opportunities. In my previous work with professional services organisations, I have found that understanding Local Partnerships' unique business model and client delivery requirements is critical. This means:

  • Engaging key stakeholders across the organisation
  • Identifying priority areas: growth, efficiency, compliance, or customer experience

Prioritisation Framework: MoSCoW Methodology

I will apply the MoSCoW methodology to help Local Partnerships prioritise initiatives and requirements. Once business needs are identified, this framework will be used:

  • Must Have - Critical initiatives required for business viability or compliance. These are non-negotiable and must be included in the roadmap.
  • Should Have - Important initiatives that provide significant value. These are high priority but can be deferred if necessary.
  • Could Have - Desirable initiatives that would be nice to include but are not essential. These are lower priority and often included if time and resources permit.
  • Won't Have - Initiatives explicitly out of scope or deferred to future phases. This helps manage expectations and focus efforts.

I have used this structured approach successfully at multiple organisations. For Local Partnerships, this methodology will ensure that limited resources are allocated to the highest-value initiatives whilst maintaining clarity about what is and isn't included in the current roadmap.

Project Governance: Gate Process Framework

I have successfully implemented this gate process framework across multiple organisations. For Local Partnerships, projects will progress through defined gates at key decision points. This disciplined gate process ensures quality, reduces risk, and maintains alignment with business objectives:

  • Gate 0 - Concept - Evaluate the business case and strategic fit. Approve or reject the project concept before investing significant resources.
  • Gate 1 - Planning - Detailed project planning, resource allocation, and risk assessment. Confirm feasibility and obtain final approval to proceed.
  • Gate 2 - Execution - Project implementation phase. Monitor progress against plan and manage scope changes through formal change control.
  • Gate 3 - Testing & Validation - System testing, user acceptance testing, and validation against requirements. Ensure quality and readiness for deployment.
  • Gate 4 - Launch & Closure - Go-live decision, deployment, and project closure. Confirm benefits realisation and identify lessons learned.

Each gate has defined criteria that must be met before proceeding. This disciplined approach prevents underperforming projects from consuming resources and ensures only aligned, viable initiatives advance through the roadmap.

4. Practical and Adaptable Implementation

The roadmap must remain flexible and responsive. It should:

  • Balance quick wins with longer-term transformation
  • Align with budget and available resources
  • Evolve as business needs change

5. Stakeholder Communication and Change Management

I have found that effective communication is critical to roadmap success. For Local Partnerships, I will ensure:

  • Regular updates to stakeholders on progress and changes
  • Clear messaging about why initiatives matter and how they benefit the organisation
  • Active engagement with teams affected by technology changes
  • Change management support to help staff adapt to new ways of working
  • Open channels for feedback and continuous improvement

Key Insight: A successful technology roadmap is about turning business strategy into a clear, actionable plan that delivers measurable value. At Local Partnerships, I would ensure this is achieved through strong governance, clear communication, and continuous stakeholder engagement.

3. Staff Adoption and System Engagement

Building a Culture of Technology Adoption

1. Communicating the "Why"

I will ensure that Local Partnerships staff understand how systems benefit them personally:

  • Saving time on administrative tasks
  • Reducing manual data entry and errors
  • Improving collaboration with colleagues
  • Making day-to-day work easier and more efficient

2. Making Systems Easy to Use

I have successfully implemented practical strategies at other organisations that I would apply to Local Partnerships:

  • Provide intuitive system interfaces
  • Deliver clear, role-specific guidance and training
  • Offer short, targeted training sessions rather than lengthy generic workshops
  • Create on-demand resources and real-life use cases
  • Ensure staff feel confident and supported
